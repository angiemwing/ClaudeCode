00 — Decision Framework

Which Palette for Which Data?

Palette type matters more than shade. The wrong palette type misleads even when the colors are beautiful. Use this table every time you start a new chart.

TypeCore QuestionUse WhenHealthcare ExamplesYour Palette
Categorical
Qualitative		 "Are these groups just different?" Data has distinct groups with no natural order. Colors should look different — not ranked or graded. Diagnosis categories, hospital systems, insurance types, patient demographics, treatment modalities 8-color brand mix — Espresso · Terracotta · Sky · Forest · Olive · Teal · Stone
Sequential
Ordered low→high		 "Does more/less matter here?" Data has a natural order from low to high. One direction of intensity. Lighter = less, darker = more. Readmission density by region, patient age distribution, wait times, medication dosage levels, risk scores Sky 50 → 900 ramp · Espresso 50 → 900 warm alternative
Diverging
Two poles + midpoint		 "Is there a meaningful center?" Data has a meaningful midpoint — values go in two directions from center. Both directions matter equally. Above/below national average, cost variance from budget, year-over-year change, deviation from benchmark Espresso (warm) ←→ Sky (cool) with warm grey midpoint
Signal
Status / Alert		 "Does this data carry moral weight?" Data communicates status, health, or performance. Audience expects universal color meaning. Never use for neutral comparisons. KPI dashboards, patient risk flags, quality metrics, outcome summaries, compliance scores, SLA thresholds Brand-tuned green / amber-gold / deep crimson / blush neutral

06 — Diverging Palette

Two Poles · Grey Midpoint

For data with a meaningful center — above/below baseline, year-over-year change, deviation from benchmark. Warm pole = above/high/more. Cool pole = below/low/less. Warm grey midpoint sits cleanly between both poles with a smooth, desaturated transition.

Diverging · 9 Steps · Grey Mid
✓
Refined — smoother transition through grey midpoint
Steps 4 and 6 (adjacent to the midpoint) are now desaturated warm-grey tones rather than full-saturation hues — this creates a natural fade into and out of the neutral midpoint rather than a harsh jump. The poles remain rich and distinctive; the center reads clearly as "zero" or "baseline."

↳ Use When
There is a real, meaningful zero or baseline in your data — national average, budget target, prior-year baseline. Both directions from center carry equal importance.
✗ Don't
Don't use diverging for data with no natural midpoint. A bar chart showing patient age groups doesn't have a meaningful "zero" — use categorical instead.
↗ Note
The grey midpoint #8C8A87 has warm undertones to stay in family with Espresso, but is desaturated enough to read as "neutral" against both poles. Steps 4 and 6 fade through grey to create a smooth transition.

08 — Accessibility

WCAG Contrast Checks

All primary text, interactive, and signal color combinations verified against WCAG 2.1. AA required (4.5:1 for normal text). AAA enhanced (7:1). Healthcare data is often consumed under stress — accessibility is not optional.

↗ Note on Div Midpoint
The grey midpoint #8C8A87 fails WCAG with white text — always use Espresso 950 for any labels placed on this step. In diverging charts this is rarely needed as values near zero typically have labels outside the bar.
✓ Do
Prefer AAA combinations for body text and data labels. AA is acceptable for large text (18px+) and non-essential UI labels. Never use a failing combination for any text, even decorative.
✗ Don't
Don't rely solely on color to communicate information. Healthcare data may be read by people with color vision deficiencies. Always pair color with a second encoding — label, shape, pattern, or position.
